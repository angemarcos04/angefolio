## Context

CSPAMS is a school project concerned with monitoring and managing student concerns. The exact implementation is still evolving, so this note focuses on design lessons from planning rather than claiming a finished system.

## What I am building or learning

The main lesson is that a concern is more than a form submission. It moves through a workflow: someone reports it, the right people need visibility, actions may be recorded, and the eventual outcome needs context.

That makes role boundaries, status changes, and record history more important than the first version of the interface.

## Current approach

I am separating the work into a few questions before committing to database tables or Laravel controllers:

1. Who can create, view, assign, update, and close a concern?
2. Which status changes are valid, and who is allowed to make them?
3. What information belongs to the concern itself versus its activity history?
4. Which notifications are genuinely useful instead of just noisy?
5. What should remain visible for accountability without exposing unrelated student information?

PostgreSQL is a good fit for the structured records and relationships I expect, while Laravel provides a practical application structure for policies, validation, and server-rendered or API-backed workflows. Those are current directions, not proof that every design decision is settled.

## Problems to solve

- Keeping permissions understandable as roles grow.
- Preserving a useful history of changes without duplicating every field.
- Designing statuses around real school workflows instead of arbitrary labels.
- Handling sensitive information with deliberate access boundaries.
- Keeping the capstone scope realistic for the time and team available.

## Next steps

The next useful artifacts are a clearer state diagram, a role-and-permission matrix, and a small vertical slice that follows one concern from creation to resolution. Testing that slice should reveal more than building every dashboard screen at once.
